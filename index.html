Research Statement

What I Work On & Why

My research centres on understanding when and why deep learning models fail under real-world conditions, particularly foundation models deployed on images that diverge from their training distribution. This question matters most in high-stakes domains like medical imaging, where model confidence must be calibrated against routine acquisition variability. Low resolution, motion blur, and sensor noise are not edge cases but structural features of clinical data.

Core question I investigate: Under what conditions does a foundation model's performance degrade non-linearly, and can we predict or recover from those failure points?

My Master's-level work has attacked this through a controlled robustness study of SAM on PASCAL VOC, evaluating segmentation performance across ~2,900 images under two degradation types: low resolution (4x and 5x downsampling) and Gaussian noise (σ=25 and σ=50). A key finding was that low-resolution degradation is significantly more detrimental than noise. At 5x downsampling, SAM loses boundary signal entirely and cannot produce coherent masks, while at σ=50 noise, preprocessing with BM3D largely restores performance. Prompt strategy also matters: a bounding box with corner points consistently outperforms single center-point clicking on degraded images.

In parallel, I reproduced the SAM-REF interactive segmentation pipeline end-to-end: dataset indexing, embedding caching, experiment logging, and benchmark evaluation (NoC@90, NoC@95) across GrabCut, Berkeley, DAVIS, and SBD. Training used a 500-image COCO subset on an NVIDIA GTX 1080 Ti. Reproduction work sharpens the instinct for what implementation choices actually drive results versus what papers claim drives them.

For my thesis, I want to take this line of investigation further: how can segmentation models be made more robust under real-world degradation, and how should they communicate uncertainty when their inputs fall outside the training distribution? These questions are especially pressing in medical imaging, where acquisition noise, low resolution, and image artifacts are structural features of clinical data, not exceptional cases. My four years at Siemens Healthineers have shown me the gap between a model that works in a notebook and one that is trustworthy in a clinical workflow. I want my thesis to sit closer to the latter.

SAM Robustness Study

Master's Research

Controlled Experiment Design · FAU Erlangen-Nürnberg · Visual Computing Lab

Designed and executed a controlled robustness study evaluating SAM on PASCAL VOC across ~2,900 images under two degradation types: low resolution (4x and 5x downsampling) and Gaussian noise (σ=25, σ=50). Built the full analysis pipeline in Python covering data preparation, degradation application, metric evaluation (IoU), and comparative reporting. All variables except image quality were held constant to enable clean causal attribution. Additionally tested six preprocessing methods for noise recovery and six prompt strategies, finding that BM3D denoising and box+corners prompting yield the best degraded-image performance.

SAM-REF Reproduction

Master's Research

End-to-End ML Pipeline · FAU Erlangen-Nürnberg · Cognitive Computer Vision Lab

Reproduced the SAM-REF interactive segmentation pipeline (CVPR 2025) end-to-end in PyTorch under reduced data and compute constraints. Trained on a 500-image COCO subset (2,849 instances) and evaluated across four benchmarks: GrabCut, Berkeley, DAVIS, and SBD. Implemented dataset indexing, SAM embedding caching, two-stage training (StageA fine-tunes the decoder; StageB trains the GFR/LFR refiner), click simulation, and full NoC@90/NoC@95 evaluation. Key finding: StageA with ViT-H improved GrabCut NoC@90 from 4.46 to 4.22. Qualitatively, SAM's IoU collapsed to 34% at click 5 after a strong negative click, while the fine-tuned decoder held at 95.4%, demonstrating more stable click-by-click behavior even where aggregate NoC does not improve.
